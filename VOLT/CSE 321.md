		2. ### Room 1: The Guard (The `test_and_set` Hardware)
			At the door stands a **Stone Guard** holding a **Bucket of Red Paint**. There is a sign on the door.
	
		- **The Sign:** Can be **Green** (False/Open) or **Red** (True/Locked).
		    
		- **The Guard's Action:** Every time someone asks to enter, the Guard _instantly_ splashes **Red Paint** on the sign, but hands you a **Polaroid Photo** of what the sign looked like _just before_ he painted it.
		    
		
		### Room 2: The Two Knights (P1 and P2)
		
		**P1 (The Fast Knight)** and **P2 (The Second Knight)** arrive.
		
		1. **P1 reaches the Guard first.** * The sign is **Green**.
		    
		    - The Guard splashes **Red Paint** on it (locking the door).
		        
		    - The Guard hands P1 a photo of the **Green** sign.
		        
		    - P1 looks at the photo and thinks: _"Since it was Green in the photo, the room was empty. I’m going in!"_ * **P1 is now sitting on the Throne.**
		        
		2. **P2 reaches the Guard while P1 is inside.**
		    
		    - The sign is already **Red**.
		        
		    - The Guard splashes _more_ **Red Paint** on the sign (it stays Red).
		        
		    - The Guard hands P2 a photo of the **Red** sign.
		        
		    - P2 looks at the photo and thinks: _"The photo shows Red. Someone is in there. I have to keep asking the Guard for a new photo."_ (**The Busy-Wait Loop**).
		        
		
		### Room 3: The Departure (The Exit Section)
		
		When P1 is finished sitting on the Throne, he walks out and uses a **Magic Wand** to turn the sign back to **Green** (`lock = false`).
		
		- Suddenly, when P2 asks the Guard for a photo again, the Guard splashes Red, but the photo is **Green**!
		    
		- P2 finally enters the room.
		    
		
		---
		
		### How to remember the "True/False" logic:
		
		Think of the **Boolean value** as the **State of the Lock**.
		
		- **`target = true`**: This is the Guard's **Action**. He _always_ tries to lock the door (paints it Red) the moment you talk to him.
		    
		- **`return rv`**: This is the **Polaroid**. It tells you the _past_ state.
		    
		    - **Return False (Green):** "It **was** unlocked. You got lucky! Since you're the one who caused the Guard to paint it Red, you own the lock now."
		        
		    - **Return True (Red):** "It **was** already locked. Too bad. Try again."

	 The Throne Room Story: The Secret Handshake

	Instead of just splashing paint, the Guard now uses a **Checklist**.
	
	- **The Guard's Clipboard:** He has three pieces of information:
	    
	    1. **Value:** The current sign on the door (e.g., 0 for Vacant, 1 for Occupied).
	        
	    2. **Expected:** What you _think_ the sign says.
	        
	    3. **New Value:** What you want the sign to become.
	        
	
			**The Action:** When you approach, you whisper to the Guard: _"I expect the sign is 0 (Vacant). If I'm right, change it to 1 (Occupied)."_
	
	1. **The Match:** If you are right (the sign is 0), the Guard flips the sign to 1. He then hands you a photo of the **old 0**. You see the 0 and think, _"It was what I expected! I'm in."_
	    
	2. **The Mismatch:** If you are wrong (the sign is 1), the Guard does **nothing**. He hands you a photo of the **current 1**. You see the 1 and think, _"Wait, that's not 0. Someone else is in there. I have to try again."_

- ## Mutual Exclusion (The Law of Privacy)

	**Definition:** If process $P_i$ is executing in its critical section, then no other processes can be executing in their critical sections. Only one at a time.
	
	- **The Story:** You are in the bathroom. The door is locked. No matter how hard your roommate knocks, they cannot enter until you leave.
	    
	- **Failing Example:** A broken lock. Two people walk in at the same time, try to use the same sink, and collide. In code, this results in a **Race Condition** where your data gets corrupted because two processes wrote to it at the exact same moment
	- ![[Pasted image 20260405081107.png]]

- ## Progress (The Law of the Open Door)

	**Definition:** If no process is in the critical section and some processes want to enter, only those processes not in their "remainder section" can participate in the decision of who enters next, and this decision cannot be postponed indefinitely.
	
	- **The Memory Palace (The Lazy Gatekeeper):** Imagine a gatekeeper who won't let you into the bathroom because he’s waiting for a roommate who is currently away on vacation to give him the "OK." That’s a failure. If the bathroom is empty and you want in, you should be able to go in without being blocked by someone who isn't even home.
	    
	- **Failing Example:** Strict alternation (the `turn` variable). Process A just finished its turn and set `turn = B`. Now Process A wants to go again, but it can't because it's waiting for Process B to use the bathroom. But Process B is "taking a nap" (in its remainder section) and doesn't need the bathroom. Process A is now stuck waiting for someone who doesn't care.
	- ![[Pasted image 20260405081130.png]]


- ## Bounded Waiting (The Law of the Fair Queue)

	**Definition:** There is a limit on the number of times other processes can enter the critical section after a process has made a request and before that request is granted.
	
	- **The Story:** You are standing in line for the bathroom. Your roommate is "extra" and keeps letting their friends cut in front of you. You’ve been waiting for three hours. Even though the bathroom is being used, you are being "starved" because there is no limit to how many people can cut in front of you.
	    
	- **Failing Example:** A "Strong Priority" system. If the CPU always picks the "highest priority" process to enter the critical section, a low-priority process might sit there forever (Starvation) if high-priority processes keep arriving one after another
	- ![[Pasted image 20260405081158.png]]
