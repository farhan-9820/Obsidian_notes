Instead of every node voting (which requires knowing who everyone is), Nakamoto introduced two theoretical shifts:

1. **The Cost of Voting ([[Sybil Resistance]]):** You don't vote with an identity; you vote with computational power (or locked capital). To lie, you must control more power than the rest of the network combined.
    
2. **Probabilistic Finality ([[The Longest Chain Rule]]):** Instead of absolute, instant agreement, nodes just agree that **the longest chain of blocks represents the truth**. The deeper a transaction is buried in the chain, the statistically closer to $100\%$ certain it is to be permanently safe.

By shifting the goal from _absolute instant consensus_ to _probabilistic consensus_, blockchain turned an impossible computer science theory into a working global network.
