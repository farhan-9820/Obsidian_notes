In blockchain, a **fork** happens when a blockchain's underlying software is updated or changed, causing the chain to split into two potential paths.

Think of a blockchain as a digital rulebook that thousands of computers (nodes) around the world agree to follow. When developers want to change those rules—whether to fix a security flaw, add new features, or change how blocks are validated—a fork occurs.

Depending on how the rules change and whether the community agrees on them, forks generally fall into two main categories: **Soft Forks** and **Hard Forks**.

## 1. Soft Forks (The Backward-Compatible Update)

A soft fork is a **backward-compatible** upgrade. This means that nodes that don't update their software can still see and interact with the blocks created by the updated nodes, as long as they don't violate the new, stricter rules.

- **How it works:** The new rules are usually _tighter_ or more restrictive than the old rules.
    
- **Analogy:** Imagine a country lowering its speed limit from 70 mph to 50 mph. Anyone driving 45 mph is still following both the old and new rules perfectly.
    
- **Example:** Bitcoin's **SegWit (Segregated Witness)** upgrade in 2017. It changed how data was structured inside blocks to increase transaction speeds, but it didn't force un-upgraded nodes off the network.
    

## 2. Hard Forks (The Radical Split)

A hard fork is a **backward-incompatible** upgrade. The new rules are so drastically different that old nodes cannot recognize the new blocks. If a node doesn't update, it gets left behind on the old version of the chain.

- **How it works:** The rules are usually _loosened_ or completely rewritten. If the community is split on the decision, the blockchain permanently divides into two separate, independent networks with their own cryptocurrencies.
    
- **Analogy:** Imagine a country changing its driving laws from driving on the left side of the road to driving on the right. You can't mix the two; everyone has to pick a side, or you get total chaos.
    
- **Example:** **Bitcoin Cash (BCH)**. In 2017, a group of developers wanted to increase Bitcoin's block size limit to allow for faster transactions. The main community disagreed, resulting in a hard fork that split Bitcoin into two separate coins: Bitcoin (BTC) and Bitcoin Cash (BCH). Anyone holding BTC at the time of the split automatically received an equal amount of BCH.

## Why Do Forks Happen?

Blockchain networks aren't static; they evolve. Forks generally occur for three main reasons:

- **Adding Features & Upgrades:** To improve scalability, privacy, or transaction speeds (e.g., Ethereum's various upgrades moving toward Proof of Stake).
    
- **Fixing Security Vulnerabilities:** If a major bug or hack occurs, a hard fork can be used to patch the flaw or even reverse stolen funds (like the famous 2016 DAO hack on Ethereum, which split it into Ethereum and Ethereum Classic).
    
- **Ideological Disagreements:** Because blockchains are decentralized, no single boss can make executive decisions. If the community, miners, and developers fundamentally disagree on the future roadmap, they split the chain and go their separate ways.
