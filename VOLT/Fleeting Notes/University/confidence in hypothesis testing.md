## 1. The Confidence Level ($1 - \alpha$)

The **Confidence Level** represents the probability that the confidence interval contains the true population parameter if you were to repeat the study many times.

### The Equation:

For a population mean, the Confidence Interval (CI) is often written as:

$$\text{CI} = \bar{x} \pm \left( z^* \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \right)$$

### Breaking Down the Math:

- **$\bar{x}$ (Sample Mean):** Your starting point—the average you found in your data.
    
- **$\pm$ (Plus/Minus):** This creates the "range." We expect the truth to be somewhere between the lower and upper bounds.
    
- **$z^*$ (Critical Value):** A multiplier based on your chosen confidence (e.g., $1.96$ for 95%). It determines how many standard deviations you need to move away from the center to be "sure."
    
- **$\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$ (Standard Error):** This adjusts the standard deviation based on your sample size. 
- Here **n** means sample size and  **$\sigma$** means ==standard deviation==
      

**Simple English Translation:**

"Start with the average I found, then add and subtract a 'safety margin' based on how certain I want to be and how much data I have. The result is a range where I'm 95% sure the real answer lives."
