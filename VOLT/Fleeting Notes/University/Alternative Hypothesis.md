The **alternative hypothesis** ($H_a$ or $H_1$) is the statement that researchers actually want to support. It represents the claim that there is a **real effect**, a **significant difference**, or a **relationship** between variables in the population.

If the null hypothesis is the "innocent" verdict, the alternative hypothesis is the "guilty" verdict that you are trying to prove with evidence.

---

### 1. Characteristics of the Alternative Hypothesis

While the null hypothesis always focuses on equality ($=$), the alternative hypothesis focuses on **inequality**. It is only accepted if the sample data provides enough evidence to reject the null.

- **It is the "Research Hypothesis":** It usually reflects the investigator's prediction.
    
- **It challenges the status quo:** It asserts that the observed patterns in the data are not just due to random luck.

### 3. Examples in Practice

The alternative hypothesis is what you write in your research proposal as your expected outcome.

|**Scenario**|**Alternative Hypothesis (Ha​)**|
|---|---|
|**A/B Testing**|Version B of the website has a **higher** click-through rate than Version A.|
|**Quality Control**|The mean weight of the cereal boxes is **not equal** to 500g.|
|**Biology**|Plants given fertilizer will grow **taller** than those given only water.|
|**Economics**|A change in interest rates **will affect** consumer spending.|

---

### 4. Why the Alternative Matters

In fields like machine learning or data science, the alternative hypothesis is often the basis for **feature selection**. If the alternative hypothesis for a specific feature is supported (meaning it has a non-zero effect on your target variable), you keep that feature in your model. If you fail to support the alternative, that feature might just be "noise" and should be removed.
